The National Development Plan promises a Maldives that restores its environment — not one that strips it away. Vilimalé's FunaVaa is where that promise is being tested.

Scroll to learn more

Vilimalé's FunaVaa is one of the last green spaces in a rapidly densifying island. The proposal to remove its old trees for housing isn't just about trees — it's about what kind of development we choose.

The government's own National Development Plan — Resilient Maldives 2045 — has already answered that question. It calls for development that regenerates natural capital, not development that destroys it. Here's what the plan actually says.

📄 Source: Resilient Maldives 2045 — NDP Zero Draft, January 2026 ↓
🌿

Argument 01

Nature Is Our Infrastructure — The Plan Says So

The NDP doesn't treat ecosystems as nice-to-haves. It classifies them as critical infrastructure — the same category as seawalls and roads. Removing old trees for housing is like demolishing a seawall to build apartments. The plan treats these as the same kind of asset.

Natural ecosystems function as critical infrastructure. Coral reefs, mangroves, wetlands, and freshwater lenses protect coastlines, regulate climate impacts, support livelihoods, safeguard health, housing, and infrastructure, and underpin tourism and fisheries. — NDP Zero Draft, Chapter 2 / Part A
🚫

Argument 02

The Plan Drew a Line — Degradation Is Off the Table

The NDP's Vision chapter includes a list of paths the Maldives will deliberately not take. Destroying natural capital is explicitly on that list. This isn't a suggestion — it's a national commitment. Removing old trees is precisely the kind of "maladaptive investment" the plan says we will avoid.

It clarifies the long-term model the Maldives is choosing and the paths it will deliberately avoid: ... development that destroys natural capital and future habitability... — NDP Zero Draft, Chapter 1 (Vision) / Part A
⚖️

Argument 03

Ecological Limits Aren't Optional — They're the Foundation

The NDP describes ecological limits as binding constraints on all development — not aspirational goals, not suggestions. This applies to housing just as much as tourism or industry. The plan makes no exceptions.

Implementation is governed by safeguards that protect long-term viability and coherence, including ecological limits treated as binding constraints on development. — NDP Zero Draft, Pillar IV / Safeguards, Part D
🔄

Argument 04

You Can Rebuild a House. You Can't Regrow Decades.

The NDP specifically warns that crossing ecological thresholds causes damage that cannot be undone. Old trees are irreplaceable. A sapling planted in their place won't restore their ecological function for decades — if ever. A housing block can be built elsewhere or designed differently. An old-growth tree cannot.

Once ecological thresholds are crossed, restoration may be impossibly costly or even irreversible. Under these conditions, thoughtful choices in long-term development are a necessity. — NDP Zero Draft, Chapter 2 / Part A
🏗️

Argument 05

Smarter Housing, Greener Islands — That's the Plan

The NDP already has an answer for housing pressure, and it isn't "cut down trees." The plan calls for land-use efficiency, densification, and modern construction methods. Housing affordability and environmental vulnerability are named together as symptoms of the same problem — not as competing priorities.

This Framework emphasises land-use efficiency and spatially informed investment decisions. — NDP Zero Draft, Pillar II / Land Use, Part D
🗣️

Argument 06

10,000 Voices Already Chose Regeneration

This isn't just expert opinion. The NDP was built on over 10,000 voices from public consultations. Maldivians — especially younger generations — expressed a strong desire to preserve the natural character of their islands. Removing old trees acts against the democratic foundation of the very plan it claims to implement.

Many citizens – particularly younger generations – express a strong desire to preserve the 'island character' of their communities, including natural coastlines, lagoons, and living spaces that define island life. — NDP Zero Draft, Chapter 2 / Part A
🌱

Argument 07

What Are We Growing for the Next Generation?

The NDP places intergenerational equity at its ethical core. Today's decisions must not deprive future generations of natural assets. Old trees are intergenerational assets — inherited from previous generations and owed to the next. Felling them for short-term housing is a violation of the NDP's core ethical framework.

Intergenerational equity is ensured through the conservation of options, quality, and access for current and future generations. It covers natural resources and biodiversity, climate and the atmosphere, wellbeing, economic and fiscal stability. — NDP Zero Draft, Chapter 1 / Part A

Imagine a Different Future for Vilimalé

Imagine Vilimalé where green spaces grow, not shrink. Where housing is smarter, not sprawling. Where every development decision moves us closer to 2045, not further away.

That's not a dream — it's the plan.
